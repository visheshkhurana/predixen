Product Requirements Document: AI-Powered Startup Financial Simulator MVP

Overview

Founders and CEOs need a fast, intelligent way to understand how strategic decisions will impact their startup’s finances and runway. Today, many rely on static spreadsheets, which are error-prone and fail to capture uncertainty. This MVP addresses that gap by providing an AI-powered financial simulation tool that lets users model scenarios (e.g. pricing changes, hiring plans, cost cuts) and see their effects on burn rate, cash runway, and growth. The system will leverage AI and web data to enrich the model with external information, and it will generate probabilistic forecasts with confidence intervals instead of single-point estimates ￼ ￼. By delivering interactive simulations, risk assessments, and actionable insights, the tool aims to help startup leaders make more informed decisions about budgeting, fundraising, and growth.

Key Benefits:
	•	Scenario Modeling: Instantly simulate the impact of hires, pricing adjustments, funding rounds, or expense changes on cash burn and runway ￼.
	•	Data-Driven Forecasts: Generate forward-looking financial projections that account for uncertainty (via Monte Carlo simulations) rather than static “best guesses” ￼ ￼.
	•	AI Assistance: Use an integrated AI assistant (with web search capabilities) to pull in company-specific data (from web or uploaded files) and industry benchmarks, and to explain or advise on the results.
	•	Actionable Insights: Get alerts and suggestions – e.g. if runway is dangerously short, the tool might recommend cost-cutting or fundraising measures ￼ – along with visualization of the most likely outcomes and worst-case scenarios.

Goals and Objectives
	•	Empower Financial Planning: Enable founders/CEOs to easily model “what-if” scenarios around key levers like pricing, hiring, burn rate, and growth, without requiring a finance background or complex spreadsheets ￼.
	•	Improve Cash Visibility: Clearly show how long the startup’s cash will last under various scenarios (cash runway) and how changes can extend or shorten that runway ￼.
	•	Incorporate Uncertainty: Present forecasts with confidence intervals (e.g. optimistic, median, and pessimistic outcomes) to highlight risk. Shift planning from asking “Will we hit this number?” to “What’s the probability of achieving our goal?” ￼.
	•	Provide Guidance: Offer AI-driven explanations and suggestions for actions. For example, if a simulation shows a high chance of running out of cash in 6 months, the system should suggest remedies like cutting burn or raising capital ￼ ￼.
	•	Leverage Existing Data: Allow users to input their own financial data or have the AI assistant fetch available info from the web (e.g. known funding, revenue, or industry metrics) to ground the simulations in reality ￼ ￼.

Success for the MVP means a founder can, within minutes, input or retrieve basic company data, run a scenario (e.g. “What if we increase prices by 20% and hire 3 more engineers?”), and get a visual forecast with clear indications of likely outcomes and recommended next steps.

Target Users and Use Cases

Target Users: Early-stage startup founders, CEOs, or small startup executive teams (often without a dedicated CFO). They may have limited finance experience and are currently managing runway and budgets themselves ￼. They need intuitive tools to forecast finances and justify decisions to stakeholders (e.g. investors or board).

Primary Use Cases:
	•	Runway Planning: A founder wants to know “How many months of runway do we have?” and how that changes if they alter spending. They input current cash and burn rate, and the tool calculates runway (e.g. Runway = Cash on Hand ÷ Monthly Burn ￼). They can then simulate scenarios like reducing burn or securing funding to see extended runway in months.
	•	Pricing Strategy: A SaaS CEO considers raising prices. They use the simulator to project revenue impact: e.g. increase price per user by 15% and assume customer count remains the same (or adjust if they expect some churn). The tool updates future revenue and shows how this affects profitability and cash flow.
	•	Hiring & Expense Forecasting: A founder planning to hire 5 employees can see how higher payroll costs increase monthly burn and shorten runway. Conversely, they can model a hiring freeze or layoffs to see how much runway they gain by cutting expenses ￼.
	•	Growth Projections: A startup wants to model different growth rates (user or revenue growth). For instance, what if growth slows to 5% MoM vs. continues at 15% MoM? The tool will show revenue projections under each scenario, including probabilistic ranges if growth is uncertain.
	•	Fundraising Scenario: The team plans to raise a new funding round in 6 months. They simulate adding a cash injection (e.g. $1M in month 6) and see how that bridges to profitability or the next milestone. The AI assistant can also pull public info (if available) on past funding rounds or valuation from the web to contextualize the plan.
	•	Board/Investor Updates: Before a board meeting, the founder uses the tool to generate forecast charts and risk assessments to communicate financial health. E.g. showing a histogram of possible cash-out dates (runway) with a 90% confidence interval highlights risk to investors ￼. The AI assistant can help craft an explanation (“We have a median of 12 months runway, but a 10% chance of running out in 8 months, so we plan to cut costs by 15% to be safe.”).

Scope of MVP Features

1. Data Input and Ingestion

Manual Input: Provide a simple form for users to enter key financial data and assumptions. At minimum, fields should include: current cash on hand, current monthly revenue, current monthly expenses, and growth rate assumptions. Users can also input scenario parameters (e.g. planned price change %, number of hires or layoffs, percentage cost reduction, planned funding amount, etc.).

CSV Upload: Allow users to upload a CSV file with historical financial data or projections. For example, a CSV could contain month-by-month figures for revenue, expenses, and cash balance. The system will parse this to establish a baseline trend (using a Python library like Pandas for CSV parsing). If a CSV of historical data is provided, the tool can auto-calculate current burn rate (average monthly net burn = avg(expenses – revenue) ￼) and perhaps infer a recent growth rate.

PDF Upload: Allow PDF input (e.g. an exported financial statement or investor update). The MVP will attempt to extract key figures from the PDF – for instance, using an OCR or PDF parsing library to find cash balance, revenue, expense, etc. (If structured data extraction is complex, the AI assistant can be leveraged: e.g. pass the PDF text to an LLM to locate financial metrics). This feature is basic in MVP: for known formats (like a standard P&L report), map relevant fields; otherwise, prompt the user to verify or manually enter extracted values.

Data Verification: After input, display the parsed values back to the user for confirmation (especially important for uploads). The user can adjust any incorrect values manually. Ensuring data accuracy is critical since simulations will depend on these inputs.

2. Scenario Simulation Engine

At the core of the MVP is a simulation engine that takes the user’s inputs and projects financial outcomes under various scenarios. The engine should support the following scenario levers, each with defined logic/formulas:
	•	Pricing Changes: Users can simulate changes in pricing or revenue per customer. For example, increase price by 20% or introduce a new pricing tier. Backend logic: The model will adjust revenue accordingly. If current revenue = Price_per_unit * Number_of_units (or customers), then new revenue = (Price_per_unit * (1 + price_change%)) * Number_of_units. For MVP, assume customer count remains constant when price is changed (i.e. no immediate churn effect) unless the user explicitly inputs an expected loss/gain in customers. Formula: NewRevenue = CurrentRevenue * (1 + PriceChange%) (assuming constant volume). The engine will apply this new revenue level in forward projections from the effective month of the price change. (In future iterations, we can allow elasticity inputs to model customer loss on price hikes.)
	•	Hiring / Headcount Changes: Users can add or remove hires in the simulation. For example, hiring 5 engineers next quarter or cutting 10% of staff. Backend logic: Adjust expenses (particularly payroll). Formula: NewMonthlyExpenses = CurrentMonthlyExpenses + (NumberOfNewHires * AvgCostPerHire) for additions (or subtract similarly for layoffs). The user can input an average fully-loaded cost per hire (including salary, benefits, etc.) or use a default assumption. The simulation will incorporate hiring at specified future dates – e.g. if hires occur in 3 months, expenses increase starting that month. (Optional: slight delay for hires contributing to productivity or revenue, but MVP can ignore revenue contribution of new hires unless user manually adjusts growth inputs.)
	•	Burn Reduction (Cost Cuts): Users can simulate reducing burn by cutting costs. For example, reduce operating expenses by 20% or eliminate a $5k/month software expense. Backend logic: Directly decrease the expense side. Formula: If user inputs a percentage cut, NewMonthlyExpenses = CurrentMonthlyExpenses * (1 - CostCut%). If a fixed amount cut, NewMonthlyExpenses = CurrentMonthlyExpenses - CostCutAmount. This lowers the net burn (net burn = Expenses – Revenue ￼), which should extend the cash runway. The simulation will show the impact of this reduced burn rate on cash over time.
	•	Growth Rate Adjustments: Users can modify assumptions about growth in revenue or users. For example, what if we grow 5% MoM instead of 15%? or what if churn increases?. Backend logic: The model will project revenue forward using the growth rate. Formula: Typically, if g is monthly growth, then Revenue_next_month = Revenue_current * (1 + g). This compounds: e.g. a constant 10% monthly growth over 12 months roughly doubles revenue. The MVP will let the user set a baseline growth rate (and optionally a different scenario growth rate). The simulation can produce two curves – e.g. one for 5% vs one for 15% – to compare outcomes. If the user has provided historical data, the tool could also compute the recent growth trend as a starting point. For churn or other metrics, MVP might not explicitly model them unless user factors it into net growth or expenses (to keep things simple).
	•	Fundraising / Cash Injection: Users can simulate a future funding event. For instance, raise $1M in 6 months. Backend logic: At the specified month, increase the cash balance by the funding amount. This does not affect revenue or burn directly (aside from interest if any, which we ignore in MVP), but it boosts cash on hand, thus extending runway. The timeline chart will show a jump in cash at that point and a new runway projection thereafter. This scenario helps answer questions like “If we raise X by date Y, how long can we last?”
	•	Other Custom Inputs: (If applicable) The framework should be flexible for “other” inputs the user might want to tweak, such as marketing spend (affecting growth), capital expenditures (one-time big expenses), or revenue from a new product line. In the MVP, these can be handled in a rudimentary way by letting the user input a manual adjustment to either revenue or expenses in specific future months. For example, a user could input a one-time expense of $100k in 3 months for a new project, and the model will subtract that from cash at that time.

All these scenario inputs should be combinable – the user might adjust multiple levers and run one simulation that accounts for, say, “increase price by 10%, cut marketing by $5k, and raise $500k in Q3.” The engine will update revenue and expense streams accordingly for each month in the projection horizon (e.g. monthly for the next 12, 18, or 24 months).

Financial Calculations: The simulation engine will essentially build projected income statements or cash flow for each period:
	•	Revenue = f(previous revenue, growth rate adjustments, pricing changes, etc.).
	•	Expenses = f(previous expenses, hires/cuts, cost changes, etc.).
	•	Net Burn = Monthly Net Loss = (Expenses – Revenue) per month ￼. (If revenue exceeds expenses, net burn is negative, indicating profit; the model should handle this, showing runway as effectively infinite if consistently profitable.)
	•	Cash Balance = prior month cash + (Revenue – Expenses) for each month (this accumulates the net burn). Starting cash is provided by user. If there is a funding injection at some month, add it to that month’s cash. The simulation should stop or flag when cash balance goes to zero or negative, as that indicates running out of runway.
	•	Runway: Compute the cash runway as the number of months until cash runs out. In a deterministic scenario, a quick formula is Runway (months) = Current Cash / Monthly Net Burn ￼, but since burn can change over time in the projection, it’s better to derive runway from the month-by-month simulation: the runway is the point where cumulative cash balance hits zero. The tool will report, for example, “Runway: ~8 months (cash out around August 2026 in this scenario)” for each scenario run.

3. Forecast Output and Visualization

After running the simulation (based on user inputs and scenario settings), the MVP will generate clear outputs with both deterministic and probabilistic views:
	•	Time-Series Charts: Display the projected cash balance over time as the primary chart. This shows whether the cash line trends toward zero and when. Key metrics like monthly burn rate and monthly revenue can be plotted as well (perhaps on secondary charts or the same chart with dual-axis for revenue/expense).
	•	For example, a line chart of cash balance for the next 18 months under the scenario, with a marker indicating where it hits zero cash (if it does). If multiple scenarios are being compared (e.g. low growth vs high growth), multiple lines can be shown.
	•	Probabilistic Forecast (Monte Carlo Simulation): The MVP will include a rudimentary Monte Carlo simulation to account for uncertainty in key assumptions. Instead of relying on a single set of inputs, the system will treat certain inputs as ranges or distributions ￼. For instance, the user might specify that monthly growth could be anywhere between 5% (pessimistic) and 15% (optimistic), with 10% as the most likely value – this can be modeled with a triangular distribution ￼. Similarly, they could set a range for future expenses or customer acquisition rates. The system will then run many iterations (e.g. 1000 simulations) of the projection, each time sampling random values for those uncertain inputs within the given ranges ￼. This will produce a distribution of outcomes rather than one outcome ￼.
	•	Confidence Intervals: The output will highlight key percentiles from the distribution of outcomes. For example, show the 10th percentile and 90th percentile results for cash runway (this can be represented as a shaded region on the cash balance chart, indicating where 80% of outcomes lie). It can also display numeric statements like: “There is a 90% chance your cash will last at least 8 months, and a 10% chance it could last 15 months or more,” etc. The median outcome (50th percentile) is highlighted as the most “likely” trajectory. By visualizing a confidence band, users can see the range of possible futures, not just one line ￼. For example, a fan chart could show median cash balance and the envelope of uncertainty.
	•	Histogram/Distribution Insights: The MVP may also show a histogram for a particular result, such as the distribution of “cash-out date” across simulations ￼. This could quickly convey, for instance, that in most simulations cash runs out in Q1 2027, but there’s a long tail of some simulations where cash lasts into Q3 2027 (if optimistic assumptions pan out). The interface might simplify this by textual output if full histogram visualization is too complex in MVP: e.g. “Most probable outcome: cash runs out in 9-10 months (median scenario). Best case: could last up to 18+ months (90th percentile), worst case: as little as 6 months (10th percentile) ￼.”
	•	Key Metrics Display: Summarize important figures from the scenario: current burn rate, new burn rate under scenario, current runway vs runway under scenario, projected revenue growth, etc. For instance: “Net Burn is reduced from $50k/mo to $30k/mo with these changes, extending runway from 6 months to ~10 months (median).” This helps users quickly grasp the impact.
	•	Suggested Actions and Warnings: Alongside the charts, the output panel should list any alerts or suggestions the system has (this overlaps with the next feature, but from a UI perspective, they should appear with the results). Examples:
	•	If the P10 (worst 10% case) runway is very short (e.g. < 3 months), flag this in red: “⚠️ In 10% of simulations, you’d run out of cash in under 3 months – consider immediate cost cuts or funding!” ￼
	•	If raising prices leads to much better outcomes in most simulations, the tool might highlight: “💡 Price increase of 20% significantly improves cash flow in all cases – consider implementing if market allows.”
	•	If hiring plans make the cash situation significantly worse, it might warn: “⚠️ Hiring 5 people now brings your median runway down to 4 months. Consider scaling hiring or securing additional funds.”
These are rule-based insights derived from the numbers, ensuring the founder not only sees the data but knows what it means.
	•	Most Probable Outcome: The median scenario from the Monte Carlo (or the single deterministic run if Monte Carlo is toggled off) should be clearly indicated as the base expectation. The UI can label one of the lines or outputs as “Most Likely Outcome” to answer the user’s question of what is expected to happen, while still showing the uncertainty around it.

4. AI Assistant Integration (Web-Enabled)

This feature embeds an AI assistant that can interact with both the user’s data and external information to enhance the simulation experience:
	•	Web Search for Company Info: The AI assistant can fetch publicly available information about the user’s company or industry. For example, if the company is somewhat known, the user can prompt: “Find our latest funding amount or press mentions.” The assistant will perform a web search (via an API) and retrieve facts such as funding raised (from news articles or databases), current employee count (perhaps from LinkedIn or press), or industry benchmarks (like average growth rates for similar startups). This can populate default assumptions or provide context. (Example: If Crunchbase or a news site says the company raised $2M six months ago and has 20 employees, the assistant can surface that, and the tool might use it to sanity-check the cash on hand or burn rate inputs.)
	•	Industry Benchmarks & External Data: Users can ask the assistant things like “What’s a typical customer acquisition cost in our industry?” or “Average burn for a startup at our stage?”. The assistant will search for relevant reports or articles (e.g. OpenView SaaS benchmarks ￼) and provide the data. These benchmarks can help set ranges for simulations. For instance, if the benchmark report says average SaaS Series A burn is $100k/mo, and the user’s burn is $200k, the assistant might flag that as high.
	•	Integration with Inputs: The assistant should be able to fill or adjust model inputs upon user approval. For example, after fetching info, it could say: “I found that your last publicly announced annual revenue was $500k. Would you like to use that to estimate your current monthly revenue?” If the user agrees, it populates the field. Similarly, “Industry data suggests a typical churn of 5-7% monthly; I’ve set 6% as a default churn assumption – you can adjust it.”
	•	Conversational Q&A: The user can chat with the AI to ask questions about the simulation results or for advice. For instance:
	•	“Why is my runway shorter in this scenario?” – The assistant could analyze the scenario difference (e.g. higher expenses from hiring) and answer in plain language: “Because your monthly burn increased to $80k from $50k due to the new hires, your cash is being used up faster, hence the shorter runway.”
	•	“How can I extend my runway without cutting staff?” – The assistant might respond with suggestions like increasing prices, finding cheaper vendors, or raising a bridge round, possibly citing common strategies ￼.
	•	“What happens if the market growth is slower?” – The assistant could run or describe a scenario of lower growth and direct the user to simulate it, or if already simulated via Monte Carlo, point to the Pessimistic results.
	•	Implementation Details: The AI assistant will likely utilize a large language model (LLM) via an API (e.g. OpenAI GPT-4 or similar) combined with a tool for web search. The MVP can use an existing “retrieval-augmented generation” approach: for a query requiring external data, the backend calls a search API (such as Bing Web Search or Google Custom Search), gets the top results, and feeds relevant snippets to the LLM to formulate a helpful answer with sources. This ensures the assistant’s answers about factual data are grounded in real, up-to-date information ￼ ￼. We will need to handle cases where information is scarce (the assistant should respond honestly with “I couldn’t find data on that” if so). The AI should also cite sources when presenting factual data so the user trusts the info, though in the app UI this might be shown as link previews or reference tooltips.
	•	Limits in MVP: The AI assistant’s web search will be focused on fetching company-specific and industry-specific data, not general web browsing. We will impose some limits (e.g. only search the company name and key financial terms, or only specific trusted sources like Crunchbase, news sites, etc., to avoid irrelevant info). Additionally, any sensitive user-provided data will not be sent to the web; the assistant will only use public queries for public data, preserving privacy.

5. Recommendations and Suggested Actions

Beyond raw numbers, the MVP will provide recommendations to guide the user’s decision-making, as a form of built-in “virtual CFO” advice:
	•	Automated Insights: Based on the simulation outcomes, the system will trigger some recommendations:
	•	If runway is below a threshold (say <6 months in median case), recommend exploring cost reductions or preparing to fundraise immediately ￼. It can say: “Your projected runway is only 5 months – consider raising capital or cutting non-essential spending to avoid running out of cash.”
	•	If a price increase yields high revenue gains with minimal impact on runway risk (perhaps user assumed no churn), the suggestion could be: “A price increase of 15% boosts your revenue in all modeled scenarios. Ensure this is feasible from a market standpoint, but it could extend your runway significantly.”
	•	If hiring plans are too aggressive given the cash situation, advise prioritizing hires or delaying some: “With current cash, hiring 10 people now is risky (runway drops under 4 months in worst case). You might hire 5 now and 5 after securing additional funding.”
	•	If the simulations show a large variance in outcomes (high uncertainty), the tool might suggest strategies to de-risk. E.g.: “Outcomes vary widely. Consider gathering more data to refine assumptions, or maintain a cash buffer for safety given the volatility in projections ￼ ￼.”
	•	AI-Generated Explanations: The AI assistant can augment these with more context. For example, if the recommendation is to cut costs, the user might ask “What costs should I cut?” and the assistant can list typical areas (marketing, renegotiate contracts, etc.) or analyze the user’s expense breakdown (if provided). If suggesting a fundraise, it might pull data on average raise amounts for their stage as guidance.
	•	Most Probable vs. Edge Cases: The recommendations should clearly differentiate between the median outcome and edge (worst-case) scenarios. E.g.: “In the median scenario, you’ll break even by next year, but there’s a significant chance that revenue growth will be slower than expected. To be safe, plan for the worst-case by having extra cash or reducing expenses.” This aligns with best practices of financial planning by probabilities ￼.
	•	Format: These suggestions will appear in a text panel (possibly with icons as noted) alongside the charts. They should be concise (1-3 sentences each) and may include an AI assistant “explanation” button for more detail.
	•	User Feedback: The user can indicate if an insight was helpful or not (for future improvement, though MVP may just log this). They can also ask follow-up questions to the AI for clarification on any suggestion (creating an interactive advisory experience).

Backend Logic and Formulas

(This section details the calculations and algorithms that power the above features.)

Financial Model Structure: At its core, the backend maintains a simple financial model with the following components for each time step (e.g. monthly): Revenue, Expenses, Net Burn, Cash Balance. The initial values come from user input or derived from data (e.g. starting cash, current monthly revenue/expenses). The model iterates forward each month applying growth rates and scenario changes.
	•	Net Burn Calculation: The system computes Net Burn Rate as Monthly Expenses – Monthly Revenue ￼. For example, if currently expenses = $80k/month and revenue = $30k/month, net burn = $50k/month. (If this result is negative, it means the company is net profitable and not burning cash.) Gross burn (total expenses) may also be tracked, but net burn is key for runway.
	•	Cash Runway Formula: Given current cash and net burn, the static runway (if burn stayed constant) is Runway = Cash on Hand ÷ Net Burn per month ￼. The model uses a dynamic approach: it simulates cash balance over time until it depletes. However, this formula is used for quick initial reporting and sanity checks. (E.g., if user inputs $300k cash and $50k burn, initial runway ~6 months).
	•	Revenue Projection: Each month’s revenue is calculated from the previous month’s value and any applied growth or scenario change. If r_t is revenue at month t, and g is the monthly growth rate (as a decimal), then normally r_{t+1} = r_t * (1 + g). The model updates this iteratively. If a pricing change happens at month m, we multiply revenue from that point onward by the price change factor (plus allow a one-time adjustment if user expects an immediate customer count change). E.g., if price +20% at m, then r_m_new = r_m_old * 1.2. The model could also incorporate an estimated churn: e.g. if user says “we expect to lose 5% of customers with this price hike,” then revenue adjustment would be r_m_new = r_m_old * 1.2 * 0.95. (For MVP, unless specified, we default to 0% churn impact for simplicity.)
	•	Expense Projection: Each month’s expense starts from current expense and changes with scenarios: If hiring X people at month m, then from m onward, add X * avg_cost_per_person to the expense. If cost cut Y% at month n, then from n onward, multiply expense by (1–Y). The model can handle multiple changes (e.g. a hire event and a cut in different months) by applying them sequentially. Non-recurring expenses or one-time investments can be treated as spikes in the expense in a given month.
	•	Monte Carlo Simulation: For probabilistic forecasts, the backend will run N iterations (e.g. N = 1000) of the above projection. Key uncertain inputs must be defined as probability distributions. The MVP will primarily use Triangular distributions (defined by Min, Most Likely, Max) for simplicity and because it’s intuitive for founders to estimate ￼. For example, if the user isn’t sure about monthly growth, they might say “min 3%, likely 8%, max 12%”. We generate random values for growth each iteration by sampling that triangular distribution. Similarly, for any variable the user marks as uncertain (churn rate, new sales, etc.), we assign a distribution. Each iteration will:
	1.	Sample random values for each distribution (e.g. growth = 9% this run, churn = 5%, etc.).
	2.	Run the monthly simulation with those values fixed.
	3.	Record the outcomes of interest (especially how many months until cash = 0, and final metrics like end-of-year cash or revenue).
We will use a random number generator and formula appropriate for triangular distribution. Example: If U is a uniform random 0-1, and distribution is defined by (min=a, mode=m, max=b), then:
	•	If U <= (m–a)/(b–a), sample = a + √(U * (b–a) * (m–a));
	•	Else, sample = b - √((1-U) * (b–a) * (b–m)).
(This generates a triangular-distributed sample.)
The simulation collects results like a list of “runway months” for each iteration. We then sort these to find percentiles: P50 (median), P10, P90, etc.
	•	Confidence Interval & Outcome Calculation: From the Monte Carlo results, compute the interval covering 80% or 90% of outcomes (e.g. P10 to P90 as a 90% confidence interval for runway or other metrics). Also identify the median outcome. For example, if median runway is 10 months, P10 is 7 months, P90 is 15 months, we have a range to report. This aligns with best practices of focusing on percentile outcomes rather than just the average ￼. The system might also compute the probability of certain events, e.g., “Probability of running out of cash before Dec 2026: 25%” if that is a concern ￼.
	•	Suggested Actions Logic: These are derived from the results: simple rules like:
	•	If median runway < X (say 6 months) or P10 < some threshold, trigger a “short runway” warning (and suggest actions as discussed).
	•	If a certain scenario parameter dramatically improves outcomes (e.g. turning on a price increase in simulation yields +6 months runway), mark that as a potential lever to pull, and have the AI assistant explain benefits and risks.
	•	If burn is high relative to revenue growth (maybe compute burn multiple or efficiency), the system can note inefficiencies. (Burn Multiple = Net Burn / Net New ARR, as a SaaS efficiency metric – MVP can compute if data available).
	•	Backend Data Structures: The model likely uses Python objects or Pandas DataFrames to hold the series of months and values for revenue, expenses, cash, etc. When running Monte Carlo, vectorized operations (using NumPy) can generate many random scenarios efficiently, or a loop if simplicity is fine for N=1000. The results will be aggregated into a structure for the frontend (like JSON containing the median series and interval bounds per month, plus key stats).

Technical Architecture & Stack Recommendations

To build and deploy this MVP on Replit (which supports hosting web apps), we propose the following stack:

Backend: Python is recommended for the core logic, given its strength in data processing and availability of scientific libraries for financial calculations and simulations. We can use a lightweight web framework like FastAPI or Flask to serve the application (FastAPI is great for building a quick API with async support, while Flask is simple and sufficient for an MVP). Python libraries to utilize:
	•	Pandas/Numpy: for data ingestion (CSV parsing) and efficient numeric calculations (e.g. Monte Carlo simulation, vectorized operations).
	•	PyPDF2 or PDFPlumber: for basic PDF text extraction if implementing PDF input parsing. Alternatively, pdfminer.six or Adobe PDF Services for more robust parsing, depending on complexity.
	•	SciPy/Statistical libraries: to generate random variables from distributions (though we can write the triangular sampling manually as above, SciPy’s scipy.stats.triang could do it).
	•	Matplotlib or Plotly: for generating charts. In an MVP, we might generate charts server-side as images (Matplotlib can save a PNG of the cash projection chart) to include in the web UI. However, a more interactive approach is to send data to the frontend and use JS charting (see Frontend below). Plotly has both Python and JS implementations and could be used for interactive charts in a Jupyter context; for a web app, Chart.js on the frontend might be simpler.
	•	OpenAI API (or similar LLM API): to power the AI assistant’s natural language abilities. Python’s openai library can be used to query a GPT-4 model (with system prompts to use a tool for web search if needed). Alternatively, we could integrate a library like LangChain which provides frameworks for connecting LLMs to tools (e.g. a Google/Bing search tool). LangChain could expedite building the agent that handles the “user query -> search -> result -> answer” flow, but it might add complexity. For MVP, a straightforward custom implementation is fine: e.g. an endpoint /ask that takes the question, performs a search (with requests to a search API), and then calls the LLM to compose an answer.
	•	Search API: We will need access to a web search API. Options include Bing Web Search API (through Azure) or Google Custom Search JSON API. Bing’s API can return JSON results including snippets; we can extract the top relevant snippets for the query. This requires an API key (Azure offers a free tier for small usage). The backend will format a prompt to the LLM like: “Using the information below, answer the question: … [append snippets]”. This will allow the AI assistant to give answers with real data.
	•	Web Server on Replit: Replit can run a Flask/FastAPI server and expose it via a web URL. The backend will have routes such as:
	•	POST /uploadCSV (to upload and parse files),
	•	POST /simulate (to run simulations with given parameters and return results),
	•	GET /chart (perhaps to fetch chart images if generating server-side),
	•	POST /ask (for AI assistant Q&A). FastAPI can also serve a documentation UI (useful for testing API endpoints).

Frontend: A simple web frontend will make the tool user-friendly. Since this is an MVP and we want to iterate quickly, the frontend can be lightweight:
	•	We could use plain HTML/CSS/JavaScript (possibly with a bit of jQuery or similar for simplicity) served via Flask templates. This is quick to develop if the UI is simple forms and charts.
	•	Alternatively, use a modern JS framework like React (Replit supports Node and could run a dev server or we can pre-build and serve static files). React might be overkill for MVP unless the team is very comfortable with it. A compromise could be using a CDN-served React and a minimal bundling. But given the time, a simple HTML form and some JS to call backend APIs via AJAX might suffice.
	•	Charting library: Chart.js is an excellent choice for simple interactive charts and can be included via CDN. It can plot line charts for cash over time and shade areas for confidence intervals. We can send the simulation data (arrays of median, P10, P90 per month) in JSON and let Chart.js render it. Chart.js also allows tooltips and legends, which is good for showing multiple scenario lines or percentile ranges. Another option is D3.js for custom charts or Plotly.js for interactive charts; Chart.js is simpler for an MVP.
	•	UI Components: Basic HTML inputs for scenario parameters, maybe a table or text area for displaying key metrics, and a canvas element for the chart. The AI assistant interface could be a text box with an “Ask” button, and a div to display the answer (with perhaps links if sources are included). We should also include some loading indicators when simulations or AI queries are running (since Monte Carlo or API calls may take a couple of seconds).
	•	Styling: Use a simple CSS framework like Bootstrap or Tailwind for faster design, or even Replit’s default styling. Keep it clean and founder-friendly: perhaps a dashboard-like layout with a sidebar for inputs and main area for charts and chat.

Stack Rationale: Python was chosen for rapid development of financial logic (leveraging its rich ecosystem for numeric and AI tasks). JavaScript in the frontend ensures interactivity and dynamic updates without constant page reloads. This split allows the heavy computation (Monte Carlo, data parsing) to run on the server, while the browser handles visualization and user interaction. On Replit, this full-stack can be contained in one repl: the Python server and static files for the frontend.

Alternatives Considered: Node.js could also handle the backend with libraries like math.js or even use Python via WebAssembly, but it lacks the out-of-the-box scientific computing power Python has (making Monte Carlo implementation and CSV/PDF parsing more cumbersome). Also, many finance libraries and AI tools (OpenAI SDK, etc.) are very mature in Python. Another alternative is using a platform like Streamlit for quick UI, but that’s more for internal tools and less flexible for adding a chat feature; we prefer a custom web UI for a better user experience.

Database: For an MVP, we may not need a persistent database. User inputs can be processed on the fly and results are not stored long-term. If we want to save user scenarios or enable returning users, Replit’s built-in lightweight Repl.it DB or just storing JSON in memory/session can work initially. If scaling up, a real database (Postgres, etc.) might be used to store user profiles, scenarios, and conversation history with the AI. But to keep MVP scope lean, we avoid additional complexity of a database unless needed for state.

Security & Privacy: We must handle potentially sensitive financial data. All computations will happen server-side, and we won’t persist the data beyond the session in MVP. If using the OpenAI API for the assistant, we must be cautious not to send any confidential numeric data unless necessary (or inform the user that those queries go to a third-party AI service). The web search queries will only use company names or general terms, so should be fine. We’ll implement basic security on the web app (HTTPS by using Replit’s default, input validation to avoid code injection, limiting file upload sizes, etc.).

Non-functional Requirements
	•	Performance: The simulation (even with Monte Carlo 1000 iterations) should complete within a few seconds. We will optimize by using efficient libraries (NumPy) and possibly limit the simulation horizon (e.g. 2 years monthly = 24 steps, which is light). The AI queries might take 5-10 seconds (API latency), which is acceptable for occasional use – we’ll indicate loading state during that.
	•	Usability: The interface must be intuitive for non-finance users. Terminology will be explained (e.g. tooltips for “burn rate” or “runway”). Defaults will be provided (if a user doesn’t know what growth rate to input, maybe a default 10% is pre-filled or the assistant can suggest one). Short paragraphs and labels will guide the user to input data correctly.
	•	Accuracy: The formulas and outputs should be accurate reflections of input scenarios (we will test with simple cases, e.g., if no changes input, the forecast should be a straight line decline of cash by net burn each month). Rounding of months for runway will be done thoughtfully (e.g. “6.8 months” can be shown as “~7 months”).
	•	Scalability: Although MVP is for single-user small scale, the architecture can be later deployed on cloud infrastructure to serve multiple users. For now, Replit can handle a few parallel sessions; if needed, we might add rate limiting (especially on the AI assistant to avoid abuse or cost overruns on API usage).
	•	Extensibility: The codebase should be written in a modular way so new scenario types or data integrations can be added later. For instance, adding an “integration with QuickBooks API for automatic data import” or expanding the Monte Carlo to more variables.

Future Enhancements (Post-MVP)

(Not in MVP scope, but to keep in mind for later versions.)
	•	Live Integrations: Connect directly to accounting software (QuickBooks, Xero) or banking APIs to automatically fetch current cash, expenses, etc., rather than manual input.
	•	Collaboration & Saving: Allow users to save scenarios and share a read-only dashboard link with investors or team members.
	•	Advanced Modeling: Support more complex financial modeling like cohort-based revenue, variable cost projections, or interactive scenario toggling (turning on/off multiple scenarios in the UI). Implement price elasticity models to predict churn on price changes, or headcount productivity models to link hiring to revenue growth.
	•	Enhanced AI Advisory: Train the AI assistant on the user’s own financial data (securely) so it can answer questions like “How did our burn rate change last quarter?” or “Analyze why our expenses spiked last month” using internal data. Also, allow it to proactively monitor uploaded data and alert anomalies (acting like an “AI CFO” watchdog).
	•	Mobile-Friendly UI: Create a responsive design or a mobile app version so founders can check their runway scenarios on the go.
	•	UI Improvements: More polished dashboards, the ability to toggle between different scenario outputs easily, and possibly a wizard to guide users through scenario setup (for those unfamiliar with financial terms).

Conclusion

This MVP will serve as a powerful yet user-friendly financial simulator for startup leaders, combining robust modeling (runway and scenario analysis) with AI-driven data enrichment and advice. By focusing on the core needs – scenario planning, uncertainty assessment, and actionable insights – the tool will help founders navigate critical decisions with greater confidence ￼ ￼. The chosen tech stack (Python backend with AI integration and a lightweight JS frontend) balances rapid development with the complex requirements of simulation and AI, making it feasible to build on Replit and iterate quickly. Ultimately, this MVP is a stepping stone toward a full-fledged “AI CFO” platform that could evolve to automate and optimize much of a startup’s financial planning and analysis workflow.

Sources: The design draws on best practices in startup financial management and modern FP&A tools, including scenario modeling features ￼, probabilistic forecasting using Monte Carlo simulations ￼ ￼, and known formulas for burn rate and runway ￼ ￼. These informed our requirements to ensure the product addresses real founder pain points in understanding cash runway and making data-driven decisions under uncertainty.
