Sharing a detailed summary of my discussion earlier today around the AI-driven business dashboard and decision simulator I’ve been building as a personal project.

Context & Origin

The idea stems from my journey at Shiprocket. As founders, we constantly faced challenges around KPI visibility, runway tracking, and capital allocation decisions. Despite using tools like Clipfolio, Tableau, data engineers, and analysts, decision-making was often retrospective rather than proactive. 

The core problem:  
Even with all data available, we didn’t have a unified, real-time, forward-looking system that could simulate decisions before committing capital.

For example, after raising $200M, a company might have 3 possible growth paths:
- Backward integration (own logistics/fleet)
- Forward integration (build new software products for existing customers)
- International expansion

Realistically, you can’t do all three. So how do you deploy capital intelligently? Traditionally, you test each idea with small pilots and then scale. But that’s slow, expensive, and sometimes reactive.

This is the gap the product is trying to solve.

---

## 1. What the Product Is

It is NOT just a KPI dashboard.

It has three major layers:

### A. Unified KPI Monitoring Engine

The system integrates:
- Accounting (QuickBooks, NetSuite, Tally, etc.)
- HR & Payroll systems
- Google Sheets
- Google Analytics / Mixpanel
- Ad platforms (Google Ads, Meta)
- Bank data
- Operational APIs

All data flows into one single real-time dashboard.

The goal:
- No more asking finance for numbers
- No more marketing reports sent separately
- No more retrospective surprises

It should give:
- Runway visibility
- Burn trends
- CAC trends (with alerts)
- Revenue forecasting
- Margin movement
- Payroll growth & cost spikes
- Cash commitments (rent lock-ins, severance, etc.)

We discussed how many companies suddenly “wake up” to 4–5 months of runway left. That shouldn’t happen. Bank balance and payroll growth are predictable variables.

---

### B. Decision Simulator (Core Engine)

This is the real heart of the product.

You can simulate:
- Revenue drop scenarios
- Marketing cut scenarios
- Hiring increases
- Product launches
- Debt vs equity funding
- Capex-heavy expansion

The engine runs thousands of simulations (best case / base case / worst case) and shows:
- Runway impact
- Cash flow impact
- Break-even timelines
- Capital required

Example:
- If revenue drops by X%, runway drops by Y months.
- If marketing is cut by 30%, revenue impact offsets cost savings.
- If launching a premium product increases burn, you may need to raise additional capital.

The idea is to “test decisions before you execute them.”

---

### C. AI Co-Pilot

On top of all this, there is an AI consultant layer.

Because it has:
- Full business context
- Historical data
- Market benchmarks
- Internet access

It can:
- Discuss strategy
- Recommend fundraising levels
- Evaluate growth levers
- Suggest capital structure scenarios
- Benchmark performance vs industry

It acts like a continuous McKinsey-style strategic partner, but grounded in real company data.

## Insights From the Conversation

We discussed a real-world example of a business operating across three verticals:

1. EBO / Store-level revenue share model  
2. Multi-brand retail (inventory on books)  
3. Large strip mall / asset development model  

Each vertical has:
- Different capital intensity
- Different IRR profile
- Different break-even curves
- Different margin structures

Currently, internal dashboards + Excel sheets are used to track:
- Per square foot per day (PSFPD)
- Monthly revenue
- Store-level margins
- Amortization timelines
- Investment per asset
- Cash reserves (~$4–4.5M)
- Growth plans (doubling year-on-year)

The key decision challenge:
Which vertical should they double down on?

This is exactly where scenario simulation becomes powerful:
- Grow using only internal cash?
- Add debt at X% cost of capital?
- Add equity?
- How much does each lever change growth trajectory?

## Key Pain Points Highlighted

1. Cash flow shocks from hiring sprees  
   Senior leaders hiring aggressively without central visibility → payroll spikes.

2. Appraisal cycle spikes  
   Deferred increment payouts creating sudden April cash hits.

3. Lack of proactive alerts  
   Example: CAC tripling month-over-month without immediate founder-level visibility.

4. Too many dashboards  
   Adoption fatigue is real. Founders only look at 5–8 core metrics daily.

This reinforced that the product must:
- Surface only the 5–8 critical founder metrics
- Give alerts, not just dashboards
- Be proactive, not retrospective
