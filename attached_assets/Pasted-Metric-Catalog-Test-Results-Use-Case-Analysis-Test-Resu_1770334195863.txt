Is It Useful? Absolutely — When It Works
Despite being broken right now, the Metric Catalog is designed to be the foundational layer of the entire Predixen analytics stack. Here's why it matters and what it's designed to do:
The Metric Catalog is a "single source of truth" registry for every metric your company tracks. Think of it as a data dictionary that defines what each metric means, how it's calculated, and at what frequency it updates. Without it, the KPI Board, Truth Scan, Alerts, and Simulations are all working off ad-hoc data without formal definitions — which is exactly why you're seeing inconsistencies across the platform right now.
How it serves the broader platform:
The Metric Catalog connects to every other module in Predixen. When you define a metric like MRR with a formula like sum(amount) where status == "active", that definition becomes the canonical calculation that the KPI Board uses to display MRR, that Truth Scan uses to score your growth quality, that Alerts uses to trigger threshold breaches, and that the Simulation engine uses as the baseline for scenario modeling. Without initialized metrics, those modules are pulling from raw data input without standardized computation logic — which explains the data mismatches I found earlier between the KPI Board ($95K MRR), the Data Input form ($50K monthly revenue), and the Google Sheets model ($95K Dec-24 MRR).
The three lifecycle states (Draft → Certified → Deprecated) reveal a metric governance workflow. You'd create a new metric in Draft to test it, promote it to Certified once you're confident in the calculation, and Deprecated when it's superseded. This matters because as you connect integrations (Stripe, QuickBooks, ChartMogul), you might have two versions of the same metric — say, MRR from Stripe billing vs. MRR from ChartMogul — and the catalog lets you certify which one is authoritative.
The two definition modes serve different complexity levels. Simple Formula mode (sum(amount), avg(price), count() with where filters) is perfect for straightforward metrics like MRR, total customers, or average deal size. The YAML DSL mode with meta, dependencies, mapping, logic, and postprocess sections is designed for compound metrics — things like Net Revenue Retention, which depends on expansion MRR, contraction MRR, and churned MRR all calculated separately and then combined. The YAML DSL also supports cross-source metric mapping, meaning you could define a metric that pulls data from both Stripe (for revenue) and HubSpot (for customer count) to calculate ARPU.
The grain settings (Daily / Weekly / Monthly) determine how frequently the metric recalculates, which directly impacts KPI Board refresh rates. Setting MRR to Monthly grain makes sense, but setting Daily Active Users to Daily grain would give you real-time product engagement on the KPI Board.
